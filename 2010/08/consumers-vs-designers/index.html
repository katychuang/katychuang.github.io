Consumers vs Designers

Posted on August 23, 2010 with labels: fashion

This is a topic I’ve been thinking about but haven’t written about, so it isn’t well developed and goes in a few directions.

Consumers of Technology & Fashion

Skinning phones

As technology grows and permeates our personal lives, we are going to see more brand and style preferences among consumers. Some common examples: Mac vs PC, iPhone vs BlackBerry. This is because there are so many options. A decade ago, you had “uber geeks” and “computer illiterates”. It was still socially acceptable to not use computers, to make jokes about computer failures, etc. Now there is that middle group of users who are on facebook, twittering, using iPhones cuz it’s the social norm. They are the middle class who pretend to understand technology when in fact they do not (it is noticeable when they expect technology to be 100% reliable). Any techie know that it’s rare for machines to be 100% reliable

Clothing Advertisement

Back in the day when clothing was not yet mass-produced, people were not choosy. “Fashion” did not exist. There was distinct social classes based on what you were able to purchase (velvet vs cotton) and how you’d invest your money. Would a poor farmer buy a delicate necklace? I think not. Then, when mass produced clothing came out the middle class emerged and we have “fashion” because people pretend they’re in a different social categorization or need different outfits for different functions. That’s the short version that skips lots of details.

I’m not trying to say that “uber geeks” are like the “wealthy class”. I’m trying to say that throughout history, new groups form with expected social norms for behavior. These new groups form because of new products (i.e. jeans, ipods, smartphones). [Aspiring] designers hope to create the product that drives the new groups to form.

Designer’s Responsibilities

These days, products are tied in very closely with brands. On the design side of things, this is what I think: technology designers focus a lot on the functionality of their designs but not so much on context. fashion designers focus a lot on context but not so much on functionality. I personally vouch for a balanced design view that looks at both functionality + context. The holy grail of design has these two and also integrates other aspects such as usability and usefulness.

Current day phone

Let’s look at a popular communication product. BlackBerry was designed for working professionals who need to be checking email often throughout the day, by “pushing” email and also offering international messaging with pin numbers. Functionality? check. Context? check. Usefulness? check. Usability? mmm depends how you look at it. It is not usable because it did not reduce work load in the long run. In fact, the normal 9-5 work hours extended because employees were expected to check email in the evening… after work hours. as if they were on call.

So.. what will the next “new” product look like or act like? Does consumer or designer or product drive societal change? Is customization a good thing in a product?
