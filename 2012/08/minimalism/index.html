Minimalism

Posted on August 5, 2012 with labels: editorial, beauty

min·i·mal·ism: Being or providing a bare minimum of what is necessary.

Louisa Boyd - Morning Mist – 2001 Watercolour on paper

Here are some notes about my journey towards minimalistic living this year described using guiding principles borrowed from web development: (1) reusable patterns, (2) exception handling, and (3) object oriented approach. ## 1. Reusable Patterns for interpersonal presentation

Since nobody notices when you wear the same 6 items for a month, minimalism is a GREAT concept for a girl that doesn’t enjoy spending time shopping, yet likes to present herself well. Apparently, French women excel at minimalism – buying only a few items each season. I didn’t think this was possible until the discovery of Comptoir des Cotonniers pants. They fit my petite but not twiggy frame. This brand is designed for ladies of all ages so it can grow with you. In fact, one of their marketing themes presents mom & daughter pairs to show that their products offer flexibility. The key is matching the clothes with accessories and your hairstyle. Cosmetics and grooming products are easy to collect, often we see women use way more products than men and become a source of humor for me [reddit]. Ladies… stop spending and start saving. No only it unnecessary to buy so many products, the bulky packaging is not kind to mother earth. Streamline with products that work in multiple ways and pack well. I get by with a small pile with items from Trish McEvoy* and Sabon.

*The Makeup Planner System

With these few items I can create couple variations of day and night looks. Admittedly, the downside to having less eye shadow colors means less looks to play with and my makeup is a bit on the plain side, but that’s ok with me, I prefer natural looks. The advantage of having few products with pre-coordinated colors means increased speed when you gotta get going! I found this incredibly useful after spending a whole day packing/moving, to be able to quickly change and apply full makeup.

2. On the fly exception-handling

New York City living is a constant stream of movement from one destination to another, made tolerable by carrying day packs for items needed when hopping into the gym on the way to/from work, carrying a laptop for squeezing in work at random moments, umbrella in case of rain, change of shoes, or in the case of an ex-marine friend – he carries enough to survive an apocalypse. My backpack has a change of clothing on days when I want to workout before work – tough for a sedentary person, but carrying so much weight on my shoulders [literally!] made me take a whole new perspective on tackling daily issues that arise.

How does minimalism fit in? When you can predict issues that may crop up, you can prepare for them and mitigate disaster. This approach reduces resources for handling crises. For example, brown bagging your lunch. When you brown bag, you’ll have more control over your meal – portion size, nutrient content, and set cost. Preparing for such an event beforehand prevents spending money on buying an expensive/fatty lunch in the city or skipping a meal and slowing down your metabolism.

3. Object-Oriented Approach to decluttering

Organizing your possessions into classes gives you flexibility to streamline your life. For example, frequent relocation becomes easier because you have less bulk to handle. For those who need a brief primer – each “object” is a collection of attributes. For example an object “bear” can have attributes of age:2, gender:female, and family:panda. In dense cities this object-oriented approach works because there are many niches.. you don’t have to know how to do everything so much as be able to find someone that has the expertise you need. Need a cup of coffee? Go to your corner coffee shop or starbucks. Need your shoes shined? Prefer to do these yourself? no problem, this likely means it’s a passion of yours that you would spend lots of time on and that makes it worth buying your own tools. What you become familiar with under OOP is following “pointers” or “signs”. In NYC there is such a sensory overload that it becomes an art at focusing on the right elements and ignoring the rest. Sometimes you have to rely on tacit knowledge to find you way around with shortcuts, just like navigating the internet.
